# Quality Standards & Review Process for UX Design

[cite_start]This framework defines what constitutes quality work at each project stage using concrete examples, review checkpoints, and approval criteria[cite: 67]. [cite_start]It aims to make quality a shared responsibility and provide designers with clear expectations[cite: 73].

---

## Purpose & Why This Matters

[cite_start]The goal of this process is to establish clear standards for UX deliverables to ensure consistent, high-quality output while balancing rigor with velocity[cite: 71, 72].

[cite_start]**Without clear quality standards[cite: 75]:**
* [cite_start]Designers waste time perfecting the wrong things[cite: 76].
* [cite_start]Quality is inconsistent across teams[cite: 77].
* [cite_start]Expectations are unclear, leading to harsh feedback or surprises[cite: 78].
* [cite_start]Over-polishing unnecessary artifacts slows delivery[cite: 79].

[cite_start]**With clear quality standards[cite: 81]:**
* [cite_start]Designers know what "good enough" looks like for each stage[cite: 82].
* [cite_start]Consistent quality builds stakeholder trust[cite: 83].
* [cite_start]Reviews become predictable and constructive[cite: 84].
* [cite_start]Velocity increases by delivering the right quality at the right time[cite: 85].

---

## Quality Principles

These seven principles guide all standards and reviews:

1.  [cite_start]**Fit-for-Purpose Quality:** Match quality to the context (e.g., a workshop artifact vs. a stakeholder presentation)[cite: 89, 90].
2.  [cite_start]**Progressive Refinement:** Start scrappy and rough, then refine progressively to a final polished state[cite: 91, 92].
3.  [cite_start]**Quality ≠ Perfection:** Quality means "fit for purpose," not flawless in every detail[cite: 96, 97].
4.  [cite_start]**Shared Responsibility:** Designers own the craft, but peers and stakeholders share the duty of catching issues early[cite: 99, 100].
5.  [cite_start]**Standards Enable Speed:** Eliminating uncertainty about "is this good enough?" accelerates work[cite: 101, 102].
6.  [cite_start]**Learn from Review:** View reviews as opportunities to improve work and build skills[cite: 103, 104].
7.  [cite_start]**Balance Rigor with Pragmatism:** Adjust the process rigor based on the risk level of the work[cite: 105, 106].

## Quality Standards by Deliverable Type

### Research Insights & Documentation
[cite_start]**Purpose:** Share user insights to inform design decisions[cite: 110].

* [cite_start]**Excellent:** Clear, actionable insights tied to design decisions; evidence-based with quotes/data; organized by themes; prioritized recommendations included [cite: 114-118].
* [cite_start]**Good Enough:** Main insights are understandable and logically organized with supporting evidence and clear takeaways [cite: 121-125].
* [cite_start]**Not Ready:** Raw notes without synthesis; buried or unclear insights; missing prioritization or context [cite: 127-131].

#### Self-Review Checklist:
- [ ] [cite_start]Can someone who didn’t attend the research understand the insights? [cite: 134]
- [ ] [cite_start]Are insights specific enough to guide design? [cite: 134]
- [ ] [cite_start]Is evidence provided for each insight? [cite: 135]
- [ ] [cite_start]Do recommendations connect to the insights? [cite: 137]

---

### Journey Maps
[cite_start]**Purpose:** Visualize the end-to-end user experience to identify opportunities and align teams [cite: 140-142].

* [cite_start]**Excellent:** Covers the complete end-to-end journey; includes all key touchpoints, emotions, and pain points; integrated analytics/research; actionable prioritized opportunities [cite: 146-153].
* [cite_start]**Good Enough:** Main journey phases and key touchpoints identified; pain points and emotions noted; opportunities visible to the team [cite: 155-160].
* [cite_start]**Not Ready:** Incomplete journey; generic (not based on research); cluttered or hard to follow; no clear insights [cite: 161-165].

---

### Problem Framing & Hypotheses
[cite_start]**Purpose:** Align the team on the right problem to solve before designing solutions[cite: 176].

* [cite_start]**Excellent:** Problem articulated in 1–2 sentences; distinguishes root causes from symptoms; evidence-based; success criteria defined; hypothesis is testable/falsifiable [cite: 179-187].
* [cite_start]**Good Enough:** Problem stated clearly with evidence; root cause identified; appropriately scoped with success criteria [cite: 189-194].
* [cite_start]**Not Ready:** Vague statement; symptoms confused with root causes; no evidence; success criteria missing [cite: 195-201].

---

### Wireframes (Low to Mid-Fidelity)
[cite_start]**Purpose:** Explore structure, layout, and interaction flow without visual design[cite: 211].

* [cite_start]**Excellent:** Clear information hierarchy; logical/complete user flows; all key states shown (loading, error, empty); edge cases documented; multiple options explored [cite: 214-224].
* [cite_start]**Good Enough:** Main flow is clear; key screens and basic states included; organized reasonably well with at least two directions explored [cite: 225-231].
* [cite_start]**Not Ready:** Incomplete flows; unclear hierarchy; only the "happy path" shown; messy files; only one option presented [cite: 232-238].

[cite_start]**Common Issues to Avoid:** Missing back/cancel actions, no error states, unclear button hierarchy, or dead-end flows [cite: 250-256].

---

### High-Fidelity Prototypes
[cite_start]**Purpose:** Test a realistic experience with users or present to stakeholders[cite: 258].

* [cite_start]**Excellent:** Polished visuals; realistic content (no lorem ipsum); interactive for key flows; accessible (WCAG AA); works on target devices [cite: 260-269].
* [cite_start]**Good Enough:** Visual design complete; real content for main flows; interactive for testing; annotations present [cite: 272-278].
* [cite_start]**Not Ready:** Incomplete visual design; placeholder content; missing critical states; doesn't work on target devices [cite: 281-286].

#### [cite_start]Required Quality Level by Use Case [cite: 298]

| Use Case | Required Quality Level |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Internal team review** | Medium fidelity, main flows, some interactivity |
| **User testing** | High fidelity, realistic content, fully interactive for test scenarios |
| **Stakeholder presentation** | High fidelity, polished visuals, realistic content, smooth interactions |
| **Developer handoff** | High fidelity, all states, full annotations, specs complete |

---

### UX Copy & Content
[cite_start]**Purpose:** Guide users through the experience with clear, helpful language[cite: 300].

* [cite_start]**Excellent:** Clear, concise, and scannable; tone appropriate for the context; consistent terminology; action-oriented; free of jargon; covers all states [cite: 304-313].
* [cite_start]**Good Enough:** Clear and understandable; consistent terminology; main states covered [cite: 315-320].
* [cite_start]**Not Ready:** Vague or confusing; inconsistent tone; passive voice; jargon-heavy; lorem ipsum still present [cite: 321-327].

---

### Design Specifications & Handoff
[cite_start]**Purpose:** Provide developers with everything needed to build accurately[cite: 347].

* [cite_start]**Excellent:** All screens and states documented; responsive behavior specified; interaction details explained; design system components referenced; recorded walkthrough provided [cite: 349-362].
* **Good Enough:** Key screens documented; main interactions explained; basic specs provided; [cite_start]Figma files organized [cite: 364-371].
* [cite_start]**Not Ready:** Incomplete screens/states; missing annotations; no responsive specs; disorganized files [cite: 374-381].

[cite_start]**Handoff Package Requirements:** Figma files (Dev Mode), 10–15 min walkthrough video, written documentation, content deck, link to research, and prototype reference [cite: 394-400].

---

### Presentations & Stakeholder Decks
[cite_start]**Purpose:** Communicate design decisions and gain alignment[cite: 402].

* [cite_start]**Excellent:** Clear narrative; starts with context (problem/goals); explicit design rationale; options presented with pros/cons; clear call-to-action [cite: 406-415].
* [cite_start]**Good Enough:** Clear structure; context provided; rationale explained; professional appearance [cite: 418-423].
* [cite_start]**Not Ready:** No clear storyline; jumps into design without context; no rationale; single option with no alternatives; unclear what decision is needed [cite: 425-432].

## Review Types & Process

### [cite_start]Review Definitions [cite: 455-514]
| Type | When | Participants | Goal |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Self-Review** | Before every share | Designer | Catch obvious issues via checklists |
| **Peer Review** | Mid-design | 2–3 Designers | Fresh perspective, quality check |
| **DX Lead Review** | Key milestones | Designer, UX Lead | Strategic direction, mentorship |
| **Stakeholder Review**| Key decision points| Designer, PO, Stakeholders | Alignment and decision-making |
| **Technical Review** | Before high-fidelity | Designer, Engineers | Feasibility check |
| **Design QA** | Before release | Designer, UI, QA | Ensure implementation matches intent |

### Conducting Effective Reviews

**Before the Review:**
* [cite_start]**Presenter:** Complete self-review; share materials 2 days in advance; prepare specific questions (e.g., "Does the flow make sense?" rather than "What do you think?"); set expectations [cite: 605-610].
* [cite_start]**Reviewers:** Review materials in advance; come prepared with specific feedback; understand the context [cite: 611-615].

**Feedback Guidelines:**
* [cite_start]**DO:** Be specific, explain "why," suggest alternatives, focus on user impact, and separate must-fixes from nice-to-haves [cite: 634-645].
* [cite_start]**DON'T:** Redesign on the spot, give subjective preferences without reasoning, "pile on" a single issue, or critique the person [cite: 648-655].

## Quality Gates & Approval Criteria

[cite_start]Work must meet specific criteria at these checkpoints before proceeding[cite: 690]:

1.  **Gate 1: Problem Validated:** Problem statement is clear; evidence supports its significance; root cause identified; success metrics defined. (Approvers: PO, UX Lead) [cite_start][cite: 693-701].
2.  **Gate 2: Direction Validated:** Design addresses the problem; flows are logical; technical feasibility is confirmed. (Approvers: Peers, Engineers, PO) [cite_start][cite: 703-712].
3.  **Gate 3: Solution Validated:** User testing shows solution works; usability issues resolved; high-fidelity designs complete. (Approvers: PO, UX Lead) [cite_start][cite: 713-724].
4.  **Gate 4: Build Ready:** Specifications complete; accessibility specified; all screens designed. (Approvers: Engineers) [cite_start][cite: 726-735].
5.  **Gate 5: Launch Ready:** Implementation matches design; critical flows work; design QA sign-off. (Approvers: Designer, UI, QA Lead, PO) [cite_start][cite: 737-747].

## Quality Red Flags

Pause and address the following if they occur:
* [cite_start]**Process Red Flags:** Skipping peer reviews, rushing to high-fidelity without validation, engineers saying "this is impossible" too late, or stakeholders being surprised at a review [cite: 752-767].
* [cite_start]**Deliverable Red Flags:** Lorem ipsum in user testing, missing error states/edge cases, inconsistent patterns, "TBD" annotations, or no documented design rationale [cite: 773-795].

## Balancing Quality & Speed

### [cite_start]When to Increase Rigor [cite: 798-806]
* High business impact or risk.
* Large user base affected.
* Irreversible decisions.
* Regulatory or compliance requirements.

### [cite_start]When to Move Fast [cite: 816-823]
* Low-risk, easily reversible decisions.
* Small user impact.
* Following established patterns.
* Experimental or learning projects.

[cite_start]**What NOT to skip even when moving fast:** Understanding the problem, peer validation of the approach, technical feasibility, and design QA [cite: 831-836].

---

## Quality Debt

[cite_start]Quality debt occurs when quality is knowingly compromised for speed[cite: 838].
* [cite_start]**Acceptable Debt:** Designing for desktop first (with a mobile plan), skipping rare edge cases [cite: 855-857].
* [cite_start]**Unacceptable Debt:** Skipping accessibility, no error handling, or zero user testing [cite: 859-861].

## Universal Self-Review Checklist

[cite_start]Before sharing any work, verify the following [cite: 982-1005]:
- [ ] **Completeness:** Is it complete for its stage? Are placeholders replaced?
- [ ] **Clarity:** Can it be understood without explanation? Are annotations clear?
- [ ] **Quality:** Have I checked against deliverable-specific criteria?
- [ ] **Context:** Is it clear if this is exploration vs. final design?
- [ ] **Files:** Are layers and files named and organized clearly?
